How much does it cost to wrap a car?

Est. cost: $2000–$5000 USD

Quick answer

A full professional car wrap typically costs between $2,000 and $5,000 for a standard sedan using quality cast vinyl from brands like 3M or Avery Dennison. The final price depends on three primary factors: vehicle size (a compact coupe uses roughly 50-60 ft² of vinyl while a full-size SUV can demand 75-100 ft²), material grade (cast vinyl at $2.50-$3.50/ft² versus calendared at $1.00-$1.50/ft²), and finish type (gloss and satin are standard, but chrome and color-shift films can triple material costs). Labor is the largest line item — a skilled installer needs 12-20 hours for a full wrap, billed at $75-$150/hour depending on market and complexity. Specialty finishes like chrome ($6,000-$10,000), color-shift ($4,000-$7,000), and custom printed graphics add significant cost. Paint correction, if needed, adds $300-$800 before wrapping can begin. Partial wraps (roof, hood, racing stripes) start around $500-$1,500.

What drives the price up

  • Vehicle size directly determines material needed — a compact sedan requires 50-60 ft² of vinyl while a full-size SUV or van can need 75-100+ ft², adding $100-$400 in material alone
  • Cast vinyl (3M 2080, Avery Supreme) costs $2.50-$3.50 per square foot and conforms to complex curves, while cheaper calendared film ($1.00-$1.50/ft²) wrinkles on curves and lasts only 2-3 years vs 5-7 for cast
  • Specialty finishes multiply cost dramatically — gloss/satin are standard, matte adds 10-20%, color-shift (chameleon) adds 50-100%, and chrome or brushed metal can cost 3-5x more than standard gloss
  • Labor is 60-70% of total cost — a full wrap takes 12-20 hours at $75-$150/hour; complex vehicles with deep bumpers, spoilers, or chrome trim take significantly longer
  • Paint preparation is mandatory — any scratches, rock chips, or oxidation must be corrected ($300-$800) before vinyl will adhere properly; wrapping over damaged paint locks in the defects
  • Custom design work — printed graphics, logos, or multi-color patterns require graphic design ($200-$500) and digital printing ($5-$15/ft²) on top of base material costs

How to get an accurate quote

  1. Measure your vehicle and estimate material

    Count doors, note body style, and estimate square footage. Sedans: 50-60 ft², coupes: 45-55 ft², SUVs: 65-85 ft², trucks/vans: 75-100+ ft². Add 15-20% waste factor for overlap and mistakes.

  2. Choose your vinyl grade and finish

    Cast vinyl (3M 2080, Avery Supreme, Orajet 970) is the only choice for a quality full wrap — expect $2.50-$3.50/ft² for gloss/satin, $3.50-$5.00 for matte, $5.00-$8.00 for color-shift, and $15-$25 for chrome.

  3. Get at least 3 itemized quotes

    Provide exact year/make/model and desired finish. Ask each shop to break out material, labor, prep, and disassembly. Red flag: any quote below $2,000 for a full sedan wrap is likely cutting corners on prep or using calendared film.

  4. Verify what is included

    Confirm: surface prep and paint correction, removal of handles/mirrors/trim (not just trimming around them), door jambs, warranty (2-5 years typical), and post-install inspection after 48 hours of curing.

  5. Budget for hidden costs

    Paint correction: $300-$800 if needed. Old wrap removal: $400-$800. Ceramic coating over wrap: $300-$600. Design/print for custom graphics: $200-$500. Factor 15-20% contingency on top of the quoted price.

When professional installation is worth it

  • You want a warranty-backed installation — reputable shops offer 2-5 year warranties against peeling, lifting, and fading; DIY wraps have no recourse
  • Your paint has rock chips, scratches, or oxidation that must be corrected before vinyl will adhere — wrapping over defects makes them permanent and visible
  • You need chrome, color-shift, or custom printed graphics — these materials are unforgiving and require experienced hands; mistakes cost more than the labor
  • Your vehicle has complex surfaces — deep bumpers, aggressive spoilers, recessed door handles, or chrome trim pieces that require disassembly to wrap properly

Additional costs to budget for

  • Paint correction ($300-$800) — Required if paint has scratches, chips, or oxidation; vinyl magnifies surface defects
  • Old wrap removal ($400-$800) — Removing previous vinyl adds labor; old adhesive residue must be cleaned
  • Ceramic coating over wrap ($300-$600) — Extends wrap life by 1-2 years and adds hydrophobic properties; UV protection
  • Aftercare products ($50-$150) — Specialized wrap-safe soap, sealant, and microfiber cloths for maintenance

How pricing varies by region

  • High-cost markets (NYC, LA, San Francisco, Seattle) — +20-35%. Premium labor rates ($120-$150/hr), high shop overhead, strong demand from luxury vehicle owners
  • Mid-cost markets (Chicago, Dallas, Atlanta, Denver) — Baseline (0%). Standard US pricing; competitive market with multiple shops to choose from
  • Lower-cost markets (rural areas, smaller cities) — -10-20%. Lower labor rates ($75-$90/hr) but fewer experienced installers; may need to travel for quality work
  • Luxury vehicle specialists (Miami, Beverly Hills, Las Vegas) — +40-60%. Premium positioning, exotic car expertise, concierge service; expect $5,000+ minimum for any full wrap

Red flags when choosing a wrap shop

  • Quotes under $2,000 for a full sedan wrap — this almost certainly means calendared vinyl, no paint prep, or trimming around hardware instead of disassembling
  • Shop cannot name the vinyl brand or shows you unlabeled rolls — counterfeit and low-grade vinyl is common; insist on 3M, Avery, Orajet, or KPMF
  • No written warranty or warranty under 1 year — reputable installers stand behind their work for 2-5 years
  • Installer refuses to remove door handles, mirrors, or trim — proper wrapping requires disassembly; wrapping around hardware creates visible seams and lifting edges
  • Shop has no portfolio or before/after photos — quality wrappers document their work; no portfolio means inexperience
  • Pressure to pay full amount upfront — standard practice is 50% deposit (materials) with balance due on completion and inspection
  • Shop is unwilling to let you watch or inspect the work in progress — transparency matters; hiding the workspace suggests shortcuts

Maintaining your car wrap

  • Wait 48 hours after installation before washing to let the adhesive fully cure
  • Hand wash only with wrap-safe soap (pH-neutral, no wax or solvent-based cleaners); automatic car washes with brushes will scratch and lift edges
  • Avoid parking in direct sunlight for extended periods; UV is the #1 cause of wrap fading; use a car cover or garage when possible
  • Do not use pressure washers closer than 3 feet or above 2,000 PSI; high pressure water lifts edges and forces water under the film
  • Clean bird droppings, tree sap, and bug splatter immediately; their acids etch into the vinyl within hours
  • Inspect edges and seams every 3-6 months; lift corners caught early can be re-adhered; once dirt accumulates underneath, the panel must be re-wrapped

Frequently asked questions

Is it cheaper to wrap or paint a car?

A basic single-color paint job ($1,000-$3,000) is often cheaper than a quality wrap ($2,500-$5,000), but a quality paint job that matches wrap finish quality costs $5,000-$10,000+. Wrapping is also reversible while painting is permanent.

How long does a car wrap last?

A quality cast vinyl wrap lasts 5-7 years with proper care. Gloss and satin finishes tend to last longest (6-7 years), while matte and chrome may show wear sooner (4-5 years). UV exposure and harsh washing are the main lifespan reducers.

Can I wrap my car myself?

DIY wrapping is possible for small areas (roof, hood, mirrors) but a full-vehicle wrap requires professional tools, a climate-controlled space, and significant skill. Mistakes are costly — a ruined roll of vinyl is $200-$400. Budget 40-60 hours for a first-time full wrap.

Will wrapping damage my car's paint?

Quality cast vinyl applied to factory-cured OEM paint will not damage it when properly removed. However, wrapping over aftermarket paint, touch-ups, or damaged clear coat can cause paint to peel with the vinyl. Always test a small area first.

How much does a partial car wrap cost?

Partial wraps (roof, hood, racing stripes, or accents) cost $300-$1,500 depending on area and complexity. A roof wrap is typically $400-$800, while hood + roof + trunk is $800-$1,500.

How long does it take to wrap a car?

A professional shop typically needs 2-4 days for a full vehicle wrap: 1 day for prep and disassembly, 1-2 days for installation, and partial day for reassembly and inspection. Complex vehicles or specialty finishes may take 5-7 days.

How much does it cost to remove a car wrap?

Professional wrap removal costs $400-$800 for a sedan, depending on wrap age and condition. Older wraps (5+ years) are harder to remove and cost more. DIY removal is possible but risks paint damage if done improperly.

Can I wrap a leased car?

Yes, wrapping a leased car is common and generally acceptable since quality vinyl is fully removable. However, check your lease terms, inform your insurer, and keep documentation. Remove the wrap before lease return to avoid reconditioning charges.
